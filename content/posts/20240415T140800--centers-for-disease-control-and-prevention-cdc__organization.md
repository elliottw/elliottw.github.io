The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) is a federal agency in the United States responsible for protecting public health and safety. It performs a wide range of functions including:

1.  Disease surveillance and outbreak investigation: The CDC monitors the spread of diseases domestically and globally, investigates outbreaks, and provides support to control and prevent the spread of infectious diseases.

2.  Health promotion and education: The CDC educates the public and healthcare professionals about health risks and preventive measures through campaigns, publications, and educational materials.

3.  Vaccine research and distribution: The CDC conducts research on vaccines, develops vaccination guidelines, and coordinates vaccine distribution programs to prevent the spread of vaccine-preventable diseases.

4.  Environmental health monitoring: The CDC monitors environmental factors that can affect public health, such as air and water quality, and provides guidance on how to reduce exposure to environmental hazards.

5.  Injury prevention: The CDC conducts research on injury prevention strategies and provides guidance on how to reduce the risk of injuries from accidents, violence, and other causes.

6.  Global health initiatives: The CDC works with international partners to address global health challenges, such as infectious disease outbreaks, and provides technical assistance and support to other countries' public health efforts.

Overall, the CDC plays a crucial role in protecting and promoting public health through research, surveillance, education, and collaboration with other government agencies, healthcare providers, and international partners.
