UPMC South Hills · Healthcare, Service Design, Org Strategy

Connecting patient experience with strategy.

A four-week research engagement at an outpatient surgery center became a strategy engagement for the system. The deliverable wasn’t furniture. It was a vertical map that turned mission, vision, and values into something leadership could operate.

Role
Lead designer · research, synthesis, vertical-map authorship
Engagement
Two phases · ~16 weeks total
Methods
Ethnography, OMI synthesis, journey + service blueprinting, co-creation, far-future remapping, vertical mapping
Context
120,000 sq ft outpatient facility serving Bethel Park, PA

UPMC South Hills sits eight miles south of Pittsburgh, in a middle-class suburb with an aging population. We were brought in to study patient experience inside a 120,000 sq ft outpatient facility. Halfway through the engagement, the corporate steering committee asked us to extend the work into a model for the future of UPMC’s satellite network nationwide. The pivot from research to strategy is the project.

— 01
Problem

A patient-experience brief with strategy hiding inside it.

UPMC’s network was expanding regionally — opening and acquiring clinics and hospitals across western Pennsylvania. South Hills was a 120,000 sq ft three-story facility with more than a dozen outpatient practices, a central atrium, a surgery center, primary care, cardiology. An obvious candidate for a model satellite. Except half the people in Bethel Park couldn’t find it, and a plurality of survey respondents confused it with the competitor across the street.

This wasn’t a UX brief on a website. It was infrastructure work for the system’s next decade, and whatever came out of it would propagate across UPMC’s other satellite facilities.

Patient satisfaction surveys had been running for years and never acted on. They told a story of broad satisfaction. Once we got into the field the story didn’t match.

— 02
Research

Layered methods, until the system showed itself.

The work pulled in just about every method we had. An ethnographic facility tour and photo documentation across every back-office, every waiting area, every storage closet. Patient and family shadowing through full visits. Surveys at the patient, check-in, and staff levels. A technology audit at the front desks and clinical workstations. Unstructured interviews with patients, families, and staff. Patient diaries. OMI charts — observation, meaning, implication — for synthesis. Journey maps and service blueprints. Co-creation workshops with sticker-blueprint and drawing prompts. And, when scope expanded, far-future remapping with image-card prompts and linguistic analysis on top of it.

A representative shadow.

We met Barry and Deborah Hume on the morning of his knee replacement. The nursing staff waits for surgery patients at a dedicated entrance they call “the celebrity entrance.” Barry’s surgery went well. He was discharged at 5 PM. Three days later, the Humes brought a birthday cake for the nurse who took care of them.

That kind of moment was common. Patients reported high satisfaction. Internal surveys reflected the same. If our research had stopped at the analytical layer, the answer would have been “everything’s working.”

Had our research at UPMC been solely analytical, only a partial picture would have emerged — patients would have looked broadly satisfied. Instead, by synthesizing across methods, the deeper organizational issues came forward.

Findings — thirteen issues at the level of the system.

  1. Siloed practices; few staff knew the full breadth of services offered at South Hills.
  2. Patients were referred outside the building for needs that could be met down the hall.
  3. Few staff understood corporate’s direction; the satellite was disconnected from the system.
  4. A clean, bright space with the atmosphere of a dead mall.
  5. Wayfinding was hard for first-time visitors and elderly patients.
  6. The central café was closed most of the time; the space felt clinical.
  7. Long waits with little for family members to do.
  8. Information flow between offices was hindered by old tools.
  9. Patients were sent to the wrong office because of missing information and bad signage.
  10. The digital footprint lacked clear listings of services and hours; no brand cohesion across UPMC properties.
  11. Finding the building was hard.
  12. Little name recognition in Bethel Park or surrounding towns.
  13. A plurality of survey respondents confused South Hills with a competitor across the street.

Far-future research, after the obvious method failed.

The corporate steering committee extended the brief: South Hills should become a model for UPMC satellites nationwide. We needed input on what “the future” should mean to the community. So we tried the obvious thing first.

— A method that didn’t work, before the one that did

We tried direct future visioning at a nearby mall on a Tuesday morning. Bought coffee for mall-walkers, talked with employees, recorded their stories. Asking “what does the future of healthcare look like to you?” produced almost nothing. People can talk about iterative change. Far-future change requires different prompts.

Far-future remapping.

We switched to image-card prompts. Four cards per question — “if healthcare in the future were one of these juicers, magazines, vacation spots, or cars, which would it be and why?” The choice wasn’t the data. The justification was.

“I like the green juicer because it’s personal. It’s one-at-a-time. With the industrial juicer, I’d feel like just another orange.”
— Bethel Park resident, future-of-healthcare remap

Hundreds of justifications. Linguistic analysis surfaced a vocabulary of comfort, accessibility, brightness, efficiency, happiness, peace, individuality, immediacy, and simplicity. From that vocabulary we constructed a map that connected the system’s aspirations to the things its workers could actually do on a Tuesday.

— 03
Process

Cheap experiments first, then a vertical map.

Cycle 01 — Direct vectors on the building.

Some problems have direct vectors. We ran low-fidelity experiments on the building itself: a warmer café, calmer waiting rooms, ad-hoc “breather” spaces with quieter music, simpler intake forms, better wayfinding. We collected feedback through diaries, sticker-blueprints, and co-creation drawings — fast, immediate, cheap to revise. The cost-value matrix below sequenced what to try first.

Design decision — stop moving furniture.

Once the steering committee scope hit, we stopped iterating on the building. Visioning at the system level can’t be built up from prototypes; it has to be designed down from a vision.

Cycle 02 — Vertical map authorship.

Vision at the top, principles below it, goals below those, projects at the bottom. Each row laddered up to the row above. The structural promise of the map was that the projects at the bottom — the things people actually do on a Tuesday — would be measurable and assignable, while still anchored to a vision the leadership could defend in a board meeting.

Things that almost broke the project.

The satisfaction scores running counter to observation was the early one. Layering methods was the answer; no single method would have surfaced the system-level issues. The blueprint plus the shadow plus the OMI plus the linguistic remap, taken together, painted a picture leadership couldn’t argue with.

Direct future visioning was the second. It just didn’t work — the mall on a Tuesday morning produced almost nothing. Switching to image-card remapping unlocked it. Treating the choice as irrelevant and the justification as the data was the move.

And then the scope expanded mid-engagement, which is the kind of thing that derails a research project at the pricing meeting. We adapted the methods rather than re-scoping, and built the vertical map specifically so it could absorb both phases of work.

— 04
Solution

A workbook leadership could operate from.

The deliverable was a comprehensive strategy workbook: research insights, prototype results, the vision-to-action map, project candidates with cost-benefit, a sequenced roadmap. Goals and projects were sized so each could be assigned a budget, an owner, a timeline, and a KPI. The workbook’s job was to outlast our engagement.

Inside the workbook, the centerpiece was the vertical map — vision down to principles down to goals down to projects, the framework that turns mission/vision/values into something a budget cycle can act on. Around it: an outpatient surgery service blueprint aligning evidence, patient actions, on-stage and back-stage contact across the full visit timeline; a Healthy Hills brand identity (typeface, color, voice, retail-ready signage) giving the satellite a name people could find; high-volume form redesigns (the reservation-for-surgery being the big one) re-set for legibility and fewer errors; and a project portfolio sized for budget assignment.

— 05
Impact

A framework that outlasted the engagement.

The vertical map went out as the operational strategy workbook UPMC could budget against. The Healthy Hills brand identity rolled out as the satellite-facility-of-the-future direction. And the framework, once we’d built it, kept showing up in other engagements — multiple visions under a company-wide vision; strategic plans inserted between principles and goals; workstreams added at the bottom for the teams who execute. The artifact has had a longer working life than the engagement that produced it.

A few things I took with me.

Layering methods is the cheapest insurance against satisfaction-survey blindness. A single-method research project on this engagement would have produced a different answer, and the wrong one. The juicer card isn’t a clever trick — it’s the only thing that worked when we asked people to imagine far futures. The choice doesn’t matter. The justification is the data.

And: a vertical map that’s pretty but not budgetable is a poster. Strategy artifacts only work if leadership can operate them.

What I’d do differently.

I’d push harder, earlier, for the strategic scope. The “patient experience” framing was already too narrow on day one. Leadership knew it. We surfaced it through the work. In retrospect we could have surfaced it in the kickoff. Now I spend that scope-question energy in week one instead of week eight.

Patients loved the people. The system around the people was failing them. Strategy was the right scope. Furniture was not.

Catapult Coworking Community · Service Design, Community, Founding

Coffee + Workspace.

A $4,000 grant, three groups with mismatched problems, one prototype that failed cleanly, and a coworking community that ran for nearly ten years before COVID closed it.

Role
Founder · designer · facilitator
Engagement
~18 months from research to handoff; community ran ~10 years
Methods
Multi-stakeholder ethnography, multi-city research, hosted workshops, café meetups, retail-space MVP, ritual design
Funder
The Sprout Fund · grant of $4,000

The Sprout Fund gave us a $4,000 grant and a mandate: improve the local ecosystem of freelancers, small consulting firms, and cafés in Pittsburgh. The constraint was generous and impossible at the same time. $4,000 doesn’t pay for a building.

— 01
Problem

An unfunded service that cafés were quietly subsidizing.

Pittsburgh was producing more knowledge-work talent than its job market could absorb. Independents and recent graduates were stitching together livelihoods in coffee shops. Small design and dev firms were trying to grow staff in step with clients and failing. The infrastructure for a coworking community didn’t exist in the city yet.

Cafés were absorbing the cost of being de-facto offices — rent, internet, electricity, hours-long sit-times — at the price of a $2 espresso. Some were time-limiting Wi-Fi. Some were covering outlets to push customers out. None had a sustainable model for the workspace they were inadvertently providing. If we couldn’t build the infrastructure that connected this group of workers to each other, the city would lose the ones it had recruited.

— 02
Research

Three constituencies, four pillars, one missing name.

Multi-stakeholder ethnography across the three constituencies in Pittsburgh, then cross-city benchmarking — out to San Francisco to interview YCombinator teams, then to Philadelphia to study IndyHall. A cultural-model synthesis once we had enough material. Workshop research with sticker-dot “have-versus-want” instruments instead of forms. And a four-lens problem framing — belonging, environment, interactions, resources — that the workshop room generated language under.

The three constituencies.

  • Independent workers were happy in cafés — or said they were. Many described it as their ideal working situation: vibrant atmosphere, chance encounters that could lead to clients, partners, friends.
  • Coffee shops were quietly subsidizing workspace. One had time-limited Wi-Fi requiring a new purchase every two hours. One covered outlets so laptops died. Several owners wanted to drop Wi-Fi entirely but feared losing their loyal customers.
  • Small consulting firms couldn’t grow at a steady pace. Client base and staff size never grew in step; when they were mismatched, firms turned down work, dropped quality, or folded.

Cross-city benchmarking.

I extended interviews to other cities. In San Francisco, a YCombinator developer described a coworking space he shared with M.C. Hammer. In Philadelphia, I visited Alex Hillman at IndyHall, one of the oldest coworking spaces in the country.

“IndyHall is all about people, relationships, trust, and business.”
— Alex Hillman, IndyHall

Alex traced coworking back through centuries-old fraternities and artist spaces — the former for its tradition of indoctrination, the latter for the simple economics of needing a big space without being able to afford one alone. Two takeaways: orientation would have to be a strong part of any service we built; and the value proposition would lead with shared economics rather than vibe.

A framework borrowed and used as a checklist.

I borrowed Alex’s four pillars and used them as a constraint set for the rest of the project — the prototype, the rituals, and the eventual handoff.

The unmet insight: no name, no constituency, no listener.

The independents I interviewed in cafés didn’t experience the lack of community as a problem. Some treated independence as a kind of superiority. But under that, they were quietly listing what they didn’t have: childcare, healthcare, legal services, conference rooms, even a shared printer.

There was no cohesive name for this group. No name → no constituency → no listener. The systemic problems they all shared had nowhere to land.

Workshop, with borrowed credibility.

I hosted a workshop. I didn’t have strong personal credibility with freelancers or with community development, so I borrowed it. The flyer listed our affiliation with the Sprout Fund (the grass-roots non-profit), the venue was a design studio that had been standing for over a decade, the aesthetic was DIY rather than clean-modern. Two collaborators joined: Kevin, a local barista who could speak to the economics of the cafés, and Laura, a master’s student in non-profit business development at Carnegie Mellon.

Instead of standard surveys, I posted demographic and “have-versus-want” questions on the wall, encouraging people to walk between them. Food from a local restaurant was free. The workshop’s second half taught the room what coworking was through stories from other cities.

One quiet finding: a strong desire to be seen and heard. The independents were a group whose superiority was independence — and whose isolation was its consequence.

— 03
Process

Two prototypes. The first one’s job was to fail.

Prototype 01 — Recurring meetups at sympathetic cafés.

We started cheap. Recurring meetups at Pittsburgh cafés willing to host. Free coffee. Conversations about isolation, resources, community. The theory was that habit would become ritual; ritual would become community; community would build inertia toward a real space.

The cafés tired of multi-hour conversations. Attendees showed up for the free coffee and stopped showing up when we moved venues. We hit a small locust-economy effect.

— What I changed my mind about

The takeaway was that the feeling of not belonging was deeply tied to the experience of space — and the space wasn’t theirs. There was no “your seat.” No visual evidence of ownership. Looking back on IndyHall, what I had read as visual disorder was actually evidence of belonging. People had marked the space, and the marks had stayed up.

Prototype 02 — Rent a retail space for one month.

With the remaining budget we rented a retail space on Penn Avenue for a month and threw a party. The party was filled with arts and crafts; whatever people made stayed up as the backdrop of the room. I made coffee for everyone who came in, which gave us a natural rhythm of conversation while it brewed.

Over the month, the same faces started returning. The $5/day rate was working. People sat and worked, brought lunches, left and came back, had small talk with desk-mates. The pressure to buy was gone. The pressure to be productive in a time-efficient manner was gone.

Design decision: belonging is space you can mark.

The single design move that mattered most was treating the room as something members could put their fingerprints on. Crafts from the launch party stayed up; toy bears marked open desks; a FaceWall accumulated portraits over time. Visible accumulation of presence is what turned the room from a venue into a place.

— 04
Solution

A coworking community designed as a service.

The room ran on a $5/day rate — cheap enough that the price wasn’t a gate, expensive enough that it filtered for people who actually wanted to be there. A FaceWall accumulated portraits as new members joined; no one stayed a stranger, no name went forgotten. When the coffee finished, a bell brought the room. Open desks got marked with toy bears, which the community then used for personal expression. Once a month, a Show-N-Tell aligned with Penn Avenue’s First Fridays gave a different specialty the floor — a natural entry point for new members. The website carried a “Hire a Freelancer” page that generated more work than the community could absorb. And on day one of every new membership, I’d collect dues up front and drive to IKEA the same day for a desk and a chair, so membership felt like arrival, not paperwork.

The four diagrams above sit on top of all of it: front-stage rituals on top, back-stage choreography below, an orient→engage→grow→launch funnel for member journeys, and the routine-vs-ritual axis the room moved along over time.

— 05
Impact

Twelve members on month one. Ten years on the calendar.

Twelve paid members signed up by the end of the one-month retail prototype. After I stepped away and made the enterprise donation-based, membership doubled in the first weeks. The operating margin settled at roughly 30%, all of it reinvested into the room. Catapult ran for nearly ten years — until COVID closed it. To this day it’s one of my most-cherished projects, and thousands of Pittsburgh connections still remember “the good old Catapult days.”

A few things I took with me.

The first prototype’s job is to fail clearly. The café meetups never became the community, but they were the only way to surface the insight that the community needed a room. And hospitality isn’t amenities. The amenity stack at Catapult was minimal; the ritual stack was rich. The ritual stack was what mattered.

The other one is harder to put in a sentence. Build for handoff from day one. Open finances, named custodians, a donation model — the design of the handoff is what made the community survive my departure.

What I’d do differently.

I’d formalize the handoff plan earlier. Written succession, custodian roster, financial transparency — instead of improvising it at the first town hall. Volunteer institutions need that scaffolding. I had all of it in my head. I should have put it on paper from the start.

Hospitality is not amenities. Belonging is space you can mark.

Capital One Wealth Management · Service Design, Alignment, Tooling

Service blueprinting for alignment.

A wealth-management product team that couldn’t synchronize across calendaring, infrastructure, design, and content. The deliverable wasn’t a feature. It was a single artifact the launch turned into a daily reference.

Role
Lead designer · service blueprint authorship · cross-functional facilitation
Engagement
Multi-week alignment sprint inside an active launch
Methods
Service blueprint with line-of-visibility, cross-functional task mapping, living-document handoff
Context
Capital One Wealth Management; multi-team launch under time pressure

Capital One’s wealth-management team was struggling to launch. The work wasn’t behind because anyone was slacking; it was behind because the work didn’t fit on any one person’s screen. Calendaring with wealth managers, the backend infrastructure that moved client data through the platform, the design requirements for the customer-facing surfaces, the content needed to fill them — four streams running in parallel, each well-staffed, each invisible to the others.

— 01
Problem

A launch that wasn’t lacking work — it was lacking legibility.

Four well-staffed functions running in parallel, none of them looking at the same artifact. Status meetings stitched together verbal summaries; ownership for cross-functional moments fell into the gaps. The team didn’t need more individual contributions. It needed a shared surface its people could read each other through.

— 02
Research
& Process

Draw the existing service accurately enough that the people doing it could see each other.

I worked with my design team to map what was happening. Customer experience above the line. Calendaring, infrastructure, design dependencies, and content production below it. The aim wasn’t to change the way the work was happening. The aim was to draw it accurately enough that the people doing it could see each other.

Design decision — tasks in context, not in silos.

Every step of the customer experience became an entry on the blueprint. Below each entry, the work it depended on across the four functions — design tasks, backend tasks, calendaring decisions, content needs. Tasks weren’t just listed; they sat under the moment in the experience they served. That single decision — ownership of work attached to customer moments rather than functional buckets — is what turned the artifact from a map into an alignment instrument.

— 03
Solution

A living document spanning awareness through onboarding.

One blueprint, swim-laned across the customer journey — awareness, consideration, conversion, planning, onboarding. Front-stage moments above the line, back-stage choreography below it. Functional teams could update their slices in real time, in place.

What it actually did, day to day: it was a single source of truth across calendaring, infrastructure, design, and content. Ownership of work was attached to customer moments, not departments. The teams updated their lanes as work landed; the blueprint stayed accurate. And by the end, status conversations happened around the document instead of through verbal summaries.

— 04
Impact

From a deliverable to a fixture.

By the end of the engagement, the blueprint had stopped being a deliverable from the design team. It was a shared instrument the whole product organization used. Status conversations happened around the document; the multi-format summary stitching they had been doing went away. The launch-readiness checks anchored on it.

What stuck with me.

A blueprint’s value comes from being looked at together. The same diagram in a deck is a deliverable; on a wall in front of a group it’s an alignment instrument. Where the task lives organizes who owns it — tasks under customer moments, not under functions. And living documents need ownership models. Naming who updates what lane mattered as much as the diagram itself.

Some artifacts move work. Some artifacts make work visible. Service blueprints are mostly the second kind, and the second kind is undervalued in software teams. A team that can see itself can usually unstick itself.

Numo · PNC · Organizational Design, Innovation Strategy

Designing a culture of innovation.

A bank-owned innovation lab had everything except an idea pipeline. The problem looked like quality control. It was actually the absence of anything worth showing up for.

Role
Designer · facilitator · process-and-planning
Context
Numo, the FinTech incubator at PNC
Methods
Stakeholder interviews, problem reframing (Routines & Rituals heuristic), prototype-and-iterate, ritual design
Themes
Process & planning, strategy, technology, entrepreneurship

Numo was set up by PNC to operate at startup speed without losing the bank’s regulatory backbone. After eighteen months we had compliance, fifteen employees, and a problem we couldn’t quite name. The first six months had been scaffolding — putting the policies in place that come with being owned by a bank. The next six was where the real expectation kicked in: produce new businesses worth spinning out.

— 01
Problem

A bank-owned innovation lab with no idea pipeline.

PNC stood up Numo as its FinTech incubator to operate at startup speed without losing the bank’s regulatory backbone. The plan: half the businesses generated would spin out as separate legal entities; half would feed back into the bank. Eighteen months in, the policy and compliance scaffolding was in place and the C-suite’s expectation had shifted to produce.

The venture team wasn’t. Leadership read the symptom as “we’re behind on idea quality” and was scoping a process project to fix it. If Numo couldn’t generate businesses worth funding, the case for the incubator disappeared.

— 02
Research

Stakeholder map, then a Box folder.

Stakeholder interviews across the C-suite — CTO, COO, Head of Product — and the venture team itself. An artifact review of the existing idea pipeline, which lived in a Box folder. A cadence audit on standing meetings, deadlines, shared goals. And finally a reframing pass through our internal Design Deck heuristics.

Stakeholder profiles.

Doug, the CTO, came from Google. He read Hacker News at lunch. He talked about “passion” and “hunger.” His instincts were startup; his caution was bank — security responsibilities at a financial institution made him conservative about velocity, at odds with the pressure to produce.

Lauren, the COO, came from the 50,000-person mothership and was anxious about ambiguity. Her instinct was process.

The venture team — three MBAs — was responsible for generating new business ideas. Their main artifact was the PowerPoint deck: financial forecasts, macro factors, competitive advantages.

The Box folder.

I asked how many ideas the venture team had developed in eighteen months. They pointed me to a Box folder.

I expected 100 to 500 ideas. There were twelve.

Not a hundred half-baked concepts to triage and group. Twelve. And no paper trail of thinking around any of them, aside from lengthy decks that felt like too much tree and not enough forest. There was no standing meeting, no deadline, no shared goal.

— 03
Process

Reframe, then prototype a ritual.

The reframe.

The instinct was to assume an idea-quality problem. It wasn’t. It was a culture problem.

The team had three of the four ingredients: business, product, engineering. But idea generation had been silently assigned to the business unit only. The designers and engineers sat quietly with their laptops. There was no cadence, no shared artifact aside from finished decks. To Doug’s point, if this were a startup there would be passion. To Lauren’s point, there were no processes.

I reached for a heuristic from our internal Design Deck: Routines & Rituals. Reframing tools work because they let you divide the problem-solution space differently — not by parts of the problem but by the lens you look through. The problem at Numo wasn’t the absence of process. It was the absence of something to look forward to.

Design decision — ritual, not process.

The intervention was a ritual. Pitch Fest. Every week the entire company would hear pitches for new business ideas — served up with beer.

The team had concerns. Quality control? Format? What happens to the ideas afterward? Practical questions; I didn’t have answers to all of them. We agreed to prototype it.

Iteration — the first one happened a week later.

The first Pitch Fest was less than seven days after we agreed to try it. Five new business concepts were pitched. They were fresh, daring, very early. Most importantly, the room felt energized for the first time in months.

We tuned the format by running it. Two minutes for an idea plus two minutes of questions felt right. We didn’t know what step two should look like — but we had more new ideas in one afternoon than the team had produced in months.

— 04
Solution

Pitch Fest, the operating model.

Two-minute pitch, two-minute Q&A — tight enough to keep energy up, long enough to surface the actual idea. Beer in the room, on purpose, to signal that this was a different kind of meeting. The pitcher became the Idea Advocate and carried the idea forward week to week; ownership was personal, not committee. Each idea got one guiding question per discipline — tech, business, design, operations — for the next week’s update. And every active idea came back the following week with a two-minute progress update. A loop, not a funnel. The cadence is what made step two unavoidable.

The diagram above shows the weekly cadence we landed on by iteration.

— 05
Impact

Six months later.

The team doubled. Three new businesses got funded for launch within the year. Pitch Fest itself became the recruiting event and the visit-day artifact for stakeholders from the parent bank. Several other rituals spawned out of it — a company-wide Excel-skills competition, hack-a-thons, a makerspace.

The shift didn’t stop at Pitch Fest. Across the organization, PowerPoint decks that had been informational morphed into pitches themselves: risk, fresh ideas, narratives of new future states. A particular kind of energy and idea expression became part of the culture.

A few things I took with me.

Process won’t fix a culture problem. A Gantt chart for ideas wouldn’t have generated the energy a weekly room with beer did. The wrong reframe (idea quality) would have led to a triage tool; the right reframe (no shared ritual) led to a weekly party. And step-two problems often dissolve in step-one rhythm — the team’s biggest concern was “what happens to the ideas afterward?” The cadence answered that on its own.

What I’d do differently.

I’d formalize the criteria for moving an idea from Pitch Fest to a funded prototype earlier. We let it emerge organically. Three good ideas funded themselves through repetition. In retrospect, codifying a lightweight gate would have unblocked the next tier faster.

The original brief was an idea-quality problem. The actual problem was that nothing felt worth showing up for. Process won’t fix that. A ritual will.

— 05 / 09
Harvey · Internal Service Design, Operations, Project Management

Designing for collective accountability.

A 35-person design firm asked for a project template. The last one had quietly died. With three weeks left in my contract, I built something different — a feedback loop that put every project’s burn rate on the kitchen wall.

Role
Outside consultant · researcher · designer · (reluctant) Perl programmer
Engagement
Three-month contract · final three weeks shipping
Methods
Stakeholder interviews, organizational read, lightweight artefact, in-place feedback display
Themes
Process & collaboration, internal services, peer accountability in flat organizations

There is one situation every designer, at least this designer, loves and fears at the same time — working for another designer. The assignment was straightforward on its face: develop a template for design projects. The firm was thirty-five people, doing client work in small teams of two to five, and there was no consistent process moving from business lead to contract through completion.

— 01
Problem

A flat firm where process kept failing on contact.

Thirty-five-person design firm. Project teams of two to five. No consistent process moving from business lead to contract through completion. Three tribes — Business People who brought in work, User-Centered People who did most of the billable work, and Graphics People who documented it — each with its own slice of how a project actually moved.

The brief was simple: develop a project template that the Business People could follow. Projects were burning budget faster than anyone noticed. Ownership of cross-tribe moments kept falling into gaps. And, as I’d learn quickly, this wasn’t the firm’s first attempt to fix it.

— 02
Research

Stakeholder interviews, until the template revealed itself as the wrong artifact.

Stakeholder interviews across the three tribes — Business, User-Centered, Graphics. An information-systems audit on the sales spreadsheet and the hours tool. And a few organizational-history interviews to figure out how the firm had formed and what that meant for the way power moved around inside it.

The three tribes.

My main contact — let’s call him John — framed the firm by its tribes. Business People brought in the work, wrote contracts, and met clients. User-Centered People put the most billable hours into the projects themselves, which ran from three months to several years. Graphics People documented every step.

Tommy, the youngest Business Person, walked me through the typical project. Someone in the firm knows someone in another company. They get to talking. A proposal gets written. A meeting with the client’s management team. A more detailed proposal. User-Centered and Graphics people get assigned. The Business Person manages everything from there.

The information systems audit.

The Sales Guy kept a serious Excel spreadsheet that tracked sales progress and projected revenue per month — each project was a row, each future month a column, a probability rating for whether the project would actually happen multiplied through. Forecasting at the front. Everyone with billable hours used a separate online tool to log time as the work happened. One system for the future. One system for the present. Nothing connecting them.

The blow-my-mind moment.

Sarah was the head of the Graphic Designers. She was the only tribe head with real internal authority — her group looked to her for mentorship; she assigned designers to projects herself. She told me graphic design was an afterthought, the Business People consistently under-estimated graphic work, and projects went over budget partly because of it.

I started getting excited about a template that could be a tool for cross-tribe conversation, not just individual checklists.

Then Sarah said something that blew my mind.

“Oh yeah, we made one of those a year ago.”

She pulled up massive documents showing project stages, with stakeholder groups listed at each one. The earlier template even suggested weekly meetings between the design team and the Business Person — exactly where I’d been heading. “Me and Frank and Tommy all spent about three months working on this system.” Tommy hadn’t mentioned it. Frank, on the user research team, was now next on my list.

Frank’s history lesson.

“You see all those people who have their own laptops? Well, they all came into the company when it was just a bunch of independent contractors.” Most of the older men in the firm had been independent contractors and just wanted to do billable work and be left alone. The org was completely flat. No titles, no promotions, no hierarchy. Power lived in tenure.

Several attempts to get everyone on the same page in project planning had been dismissed. The firm’s flatness wasn’t a value statement so much as a residue of how the firm formed.

Process could not enter through the front door.
— 03
Process

Don’t make a template. Make the data unavoidable.

— What I changed my mind about

I had three weeks left on my contract and a brief that, if completed as scoped, would be the firm’s third or fourth attempt at the same artifact. The job wasn’t to make a better template. It was to make a thing the firm wouldn’t need anyone to enforce.

Design decision — bridge two systems that already existed.

The Sales Guy’s spreadsheet projected revenue per project per month. The hours tool recorded what people had actually billed. Both were quietly accurate. Neither was visible to anyone outside its respective tribe.

If a project was supposed to burn 100k over four months and was at 60k after two, the Business Person managing it should know, the design team should adjust hours, and the rest of the firm should be able to help if they could. The two systems already had everything needed to do that math. They just hadn’t been talking to each other.

Tools.

Perl. Yes, Perl. The systems I needed to read from were what they were, and Perl was what I had on the laptop that knew them. HTTP requests against the hours-tool API. A CSV export of the sales spreadsheet. One rendered HTML page for the kitchen wall.

Things that almost broke the project.

Three constraints, all biting at the same time. No authority to introduce process — solved by skipping process entirely; visibility doesn’t need authority. Two siloed data sources owned by different tribes — joined them in code, rather than asking either tribe to change how they worked. And three weeks until the contract ended — stripped scope to one screen, one number, one place in the office.

— 04
Solution

A kitchen-wall dashboard the room enforced on its own.

Every adult in the firm went into the kitchen multiple times a day. It was where informal conversation happened. It was the only space where every tribe overlapped. A dashboard there was not a place anyone had to be sent to; it was a place everyone passed through anyway. The artifact didn’t need a champion. The room did the enforcement.

The thing itself was small and direct. One row per active project, generated daily. Two numbers per row — where the project should be by today, and where it actually was. A red bar if the second number had passed the first. No nuance. No commentary. The bar was the alert. Tommy didn’t need to be told his project was 10k over. He could see it on his way to the coffee maker, next to everyone else’s.

If process can’t enter through the front door of a flat firm, visibility can. Nobody had to grant me authority to put numbers on a wall.

— 05
Impact

Validated in a single CFO question.

I walked over to the CFO’s office and showed her a chart of every project currently going on. She asked one question.

“Why is that one red?”
— The CFO, on first look

I answered. She set a meeting. The chart went up in the kitchen. It was the firm’s first project-tracking artifact that wasn’t quietly ignored.

A few things I took with me.

Process needs authority. Visibility doesn’t. In flat organizations the cheapest, most durable intervention is often a screen on a wall everyone walks past. Bridge the data before you try to change behavior — both source systems were already accurate; the intervention was the join, not new tooling. And place is half the design. The kitchen wasn’t incidental. It was the design move.

What I’d do differently.

I’d hand off the codebase more carefully. The Perl script was a one-person artifact, and three weeks left no time to document it for someone else to maintain. A kitchen dashboard that breaks silently a year later isn’t durable enough.

Process needs authority. Visibility doesn’t. In flat organizations the cheapest, most durable intervention is often a screen on a wall everyone walks past.

LegalSifter · Product UX, Pivot, Founding

From consumer app to B2B.

A contracts-AI startup. A loud, fast TechCrunch launch the legal community hated. A pivot to enterprise that survived on a screen-recording study, a three-pane application, and the willingness to ship something that wasn’t pretty.

Role
Co-founder · designer · CEO duties shared
Engagement
Multi-year founding through first ARR contract
Methods
Founder-led research, scorecard rubric design, NLP+ontology product strategy, screen-recording analysis, three-pane interface design, time-and-motion quantification
First ARR client
BNY Mellon — Dodd-Frank contract review

After running a design firm for less than a year, I’d signed contracts I shouldn’t have. The clauses that bit us weren’t the loud ones. They were the quiet ones. “Payable no later than net 30 days to Consultant, upon completion … and subject to test and acceptance.” The word that got us was acceptance. Six months after delivery, contracts of ours were still “in acceptance.” After enough conversations with a friend inside that company, I learned several consulting contracts had been in that state for over two years.

— 01
Problem

A me-problem that turned out to be a market problem.

Quiet contract clauses were quietly bankrupting small firms. Mine was one of them. Several of our consulting contracts had been “in acceptance” for over two years; the clause that did the damage wasn’t the loud one. It was “subject to test and acceptance” with no acceptance window defined.

Contract review is the highest-leverage moment in any client engagement. Most small businesses can’t afford a full legal review on every contract. The market for tooling that bridged that gap was real — and at enterprise scale we estimated it at $8B in labor.

— 02
Research

Same shape of problem, three different scales.

Founder-led interviews through my contact list, bucketed by company size. Enterprise prototype engagements with Giant Eagle and FedEx Ground. Screen-recording analysis of operator workflows when we got to BNYM later. And a parallel track of technical feasibility research — PDF processing, NLP, inference engines — so the design wasn’t outrunning what could actually be built.

User profiles — what each scale was asking for.

Startups asked:

  • Should I sign this contract?
  • When does IP transfer on this project?
  • Who needs to sign the project NDA?
  • Does my lawyer know the current trends?

SMBs asked:

  • Are we taking on too much liability?
  • Which contracts auto-renew?
  • Which projects have confidentiality clauses?
  • Which contracts have the standard assignment clauses?

Enterprise asked:

  • Where are our highest-risk areas?
  • How many contracts are we currently in?
  • How do we manage liability across business units?
  • How many leases are up for renewal this year?

Technical feasibility, in parallel.

  • Scanned PDFs: post-processing rules and heuristics to clean up poor scans and fill in missing data.
  • NLP: a semantic approach, growing ontologies iteratively, with a storage format that supports a later inference engine.
  • Inference: Apache Jena or Sesame as the inference layer.
— 03
Process

Two cycles, one ugly pivot, one durable design decision.

Cycle 01 — B2C, fast and wrong.

We led with values. Partnered with Gabe Levine — he and Mike Monteiro had coined “F*** You Pay Me” in the design world. Gabe scored contracts by hand; his rubric trained the model. Users uploaded contracts and got back a Scorecard within seconds: extracted terms, a favorability grade, expert advice. We rushed a polished web app out for a TechCrunch launch.

Day one: 4,000 contracts uploaded. The freelance design and dev community loved it. The legal community did not. TechCrunch shut down the comments section. Gabe pulled out. Our defense law firm dropped us.

We had five full-time employees, a viral consumer audience, and no business.

Cycle 02 — Pivot to enterprise.

A key investor had friends at BNY Mellon dealing with the same contract problem at scale. The Dodd-Frank Act deadline was approaching; large banks had spent years failing to wrangle their contract portfolios. BNYM had a team in Pune, India working through tens of thousands of deals by hand.

We watched hours of screen recordings of the Pune team to learn the exact workflow. We had time-and-motion data because of those recordings, so we could quantify savings before deployment.

— What I changed my mind about

Our first instinct was to ship the AI’s output as the answer. Watching the recordings, it was obvious that BNYM’s team would not — and should not — accept output straight from a small AI startup at face value. The interface had to make the source visible at all times. Trust would have to be designed in.

Design decision — three panes, one click.

Source PDF on the left. Identified legal terms in the middle. NLP-extracted values on the right. Click any value, any term, any span: jump to its exact location in the source PDF. Trust comes from one-click verification.

Things that almost broke the project.

The public-launch implosion was the obvious one. Same tech, different customer, different trust model — that was the pivot. The black-box AI problem was harder: in a domain where the cost of being wrong is enormous, asking users to trust the model is the wrong design move. We made the source verifiable in the UI instead. And the third was the boring one — pixel perfection versus shipping. We shipped working software so the company survived another day. Aesthetic perfection lost.

— 04
Solution

Three-pane contract review with one-click traceability.

The system ingested tens of thousands of contracts, automatically extracted common terms like Governing Jurisdiction and Start Date, and gave suggested interpretations of more complex legal language. The diagram above shows the operating layout; the production app rendered it in the BNYM brand.

Three panes — source PDF, extracted legal terms, NLP-extracted values, side by side. Every claim and every value linked back to its exact span in the source document; one click jumped you there. Terms with non-standard or open-ended language got flagged with a suggested action. And the whole thing was engineered for a portfolio, not one-off review — tens of thousands of contracts moved through it.

— 05
Impact

First real ARR. Startup survived.

BNY Mellon signed a one-year contract — LegalSifter’s first real ARR. The consumer launch had its own kind of validation: 4,000 contracts uploaded on day one, viral even though the audience was the wrong one. And the enterprise deployment moved tens of thousands of contracts through the pipeline.

A few things I took with me.

The market doesn’t care which version tasted better. The TechCrunch launch was fast, loud, and wrong. The Pune build was slow, ugly, and right. Trust in AI products is a UI design problem — one-click traceability mattered more than the model’s accuracy. And: pivot scope, not values. Same tech, same problem, different customer. Pivoting the audience was cheaper than rebuilding.

What I’d do differently.

Legal-domain stakeholder research, before the consumer launch instead of after. The Scorecard’s underlying assumption about who gets to assess a contract was the source of the backlash. A handful of conversations with practicing attorneys would have surfaced the risk before TechCrunch did.

The TechCrunch launch was fast, loud, and wrong. The Pune build was slow, ugly, and right. The market doesn’t care which one tasted better.

Innovu · Product UX, Contextual Design, B2B Research

Research at the level of the data model.

A B2B BI tool for self-insured companies. Contextual design research that surfaced what 84% of users were actually doing — and what the platform’s next version needed to be.

Role
Lead designer · research lead · partnered with one UI designer
Engagement
Multi-month research engagement followed by a near-term UI redesign
Methods
Design-brief facilitation, Contextual Design interviews, breakdown analysis, How-Might-We synthesis, low-fi wireframes, home-screen redesign
Context
100-person engineering- and data-led firm; first design hires; complex B2B product

A 100-person company hired its first designers. The product had been built as a 1:1 visual representation of its underlying data model — a fine UI for the engineers who built it, a hard one for the customers who were now using it. We were brought in to understand what was happening on the customer side.

The product itself is niche. Most companies outsource health insurance to a provider; the ones that don’t pay claims out of their own ledger and need a way to track those claims across an employee population. Self-insured companies use this kind of platform to figure out what’s costing the most, so they can intervene — switch a prescription drug provider, pre-empt a recurring high-cost procedure pattern. The platform was built to support that work. The question was whether it actually did.

— 01
Problem

A B2B product that had outgrown the customers it was originally built for.

The product had been built as a near-1:1 visual representation of its underlying data model — a fine UI for the engineers who built it, a hard one for the customers who were now using it. As the company moved its center of gravity toward external clients, more layers of abstraction got added every time a customer asked a question that didn’t have a pre-built dashboard. The dashboard list grew into the thousands. Customer Service was the de facto search engine.

Customers were spending more time hunting for the right dashboard than analyzing the data inside it. Anything that looked like a custom question required a phone call. The casual users the company wanted to grow into — benefits managers at self-insured companies — were getting the worst of it. The product’s next round of growth depended on the casual user. The casual user was the one being failed.

— 02
Research

Building the brief from zero, then doing the field work.

Design-brief facilitation across leadership, sales, support, and engineering — the 10-point template below. Contextual Design interviews with active customers, dozens of them over video. Breakdown analysis and cultural-model synthesis afterward. How-Might-We statements once the patterns sorted themselves out. And, separately, an end-to-end design pipeline demonstration so the engineering-and-data-led team could watch one interview turn into a wireframe.

Step one — build the design brief.

The first thing I do on a project like this is ask for the design brief. Most teams don’t have one because no one’s ever asked everyone — leadership, sales, support, engineering — what they actually want from the project. So I built one.

The 10-point template I used:

  • Users and customers
  • Problems
  • Solutions today
  • Business challenge
  • Solution ideas
  • Budget
  • Adoption strategy
  • User metrics
  • Business impact
  • Anticipated risks

After a handful of leadership calls, two things came back. The team had identified a focus — “Casual Users” — but didn’t know how many there were, how often they logged in, or what their job titles were. And one stat that, at the time, was treated as low-priority: an “extremely low volume” of users pulled data out of the platform to perform calculations in Excel. The very task the BI tool was built to make unnecessary.

— The first hypothesis worth testing

If even a small fraction of users were exporting to Excel to do the work the platform was supposed to do, the platform was paying its developers to build something its users were quietly working around. The number was the lead.

Step two — Contextual Design with the people who actually use the product.

I was working with a UI designer without research experience. Contextual design was the right fit — well-documented, learnable from the inside, and produces models a team can read. We built a lightweight interview template, the PM lined up participants, and we ran dozens of video calls.

User profile — the broker’s NPI hunt.

A representative interview was a broker who’d started his own brokerage. Tech-savvy. He was trying to find out which claims his client (a healthcare provider) was sending out instead of performing in-house. A perfect job for the platform.

Except his client operated under multiple legal names — Something-Something Orthopedic, Something-Something Medical — and each name had its own unique NPI number. The broker didn’t know what an NPI number was called when he started. Once he found the NPIs, he could search the platform for matching claims.

Except the platform returned thousands of duplicates, and the export only carried the first 500 rows. So he exported anyway, switched to Python to dedupe, came back to the platform, ran a second search against the unique IDs, then filtered down to his client’s ones — and added it all up by hand.

His full process, written out:

  1. Find the NPI codes for each of the client’s legal names.
  2. Search the database for claims associated with those IDs.
  3. Create a list of claim type IDs and remove duplicates.
  4. Search the database for the claim type IDs.
  5. Filter out claims that had the client IDs.
  6. Sum up what was being left on the table.

Steps 1 and 3 happened outside the platform. The platform’s contribution was searches 2 and 4. The other two-thirds of the work was Python and grit.

— 03
Process

From transcript to wireframe, end to end.

We took that one interview and walked the team through the full Contextual Design pipeline: interview transcript → unsorted findings → models for data flow, sequence of events, and cultural pressures → How-Might-We statements → wireframes inside the product’s existing aesthetic. Showing the chain end-to-end made the methodology concrete instead of abstract for a team that hadn’t worked with a designer before.

“I would be fearful if [working in the platform] would convey incompetence. I’d rather use Python.”
— The broker, on why his preferred tool was a programming language and not the BI platform

The broker’s clients wanted to see the underlying work. He believed platform-based workflows would expose how much hand-work was actually happening, and he kept doing the work in Python where competence reads. The platform’s UX wasn’t fighting Excel. It was fighting professional pride.

How-Might-We statements.

  • Help users skip the code-based middle steps.
  • Turn insights into composable Data Journals.
  • Move from guess-and-check to embedded muscle memory.
  • Make easy things discoverable.

One of those — “skip the code-based middle steps” — turned into a wireframe of a search pattern: instead of searching within a field, start with a piece of data and find every field where that data exists. A simple inversion. Useful when the data is known but the field name isn’t (which is the broker’s whole opening problem with the NPI number).

— 03b
Findings

Two insights at the level of strategy.

Most contextual research surfaces dozens of small breakdowns. Two of ours pulled rank.

Insight 01 — Cascading data issues.

The platform’s UI exposed the data model directly. The data model included duplicates from upstream feeds. The UI capped query results at 500 rows, so users couldn’t even see the duplicates without exporting. And the UI didn’t allow data editing, so there was no way to fix the duplicates from inside the platform. A simple question — how many claims did we have in April? — required leaving the tool to answer.

Insight 02 — Extract-Transform-Load.

ETL is a textbook pattern in data engineering — extract the data, transform it, load it somewhere else. Users were performing the entire ETL pattern outside the BI tool: extract via export, transform in Excel or Python, load into PowerPoint to share. The original brief had called this “extremely low volume” behavior.

In our research, 84% of participants used some form of ETL in their regular work.

The 84% number isn’t a problem statement. It’s a strategy statement. It tells you what the next version of the product needs to do.

— 04
Solution

The roadmap that shipped.

We presented the research to leadership. The bigger product concept — bringing the ETL pattern back inside the platform — was scoped to a future visioning cycle. Near-term, we shipped what we could from the research itself: a redesigned home screen that surfaced the views users actually navigated to most, lighter-weight templates, and a “Levels of the System” navigation pattern that read in the existing visual language.

Up close, that meant a home-screen reset that surfaced the most-navigated views and removed the dashboard-name guessing game. The Levels of the System pattern turned existing marketing vocabulary into a navigation primitive — the team had been saying these words in decks for a year, so we made them a UI element. A chip component carried the Levels pattern across the product. And the most-common query templates got lighter, with less ceremony around running them.

These were pragmatic wins. The research had identified bigger structural opportunities; the home-screen work made the things in front of users measurably easier in the meantime. I rolled off as the team transitioned to a visual-design-led phase, with the research findings sitting in the roadmap to feed the next product cycle.

— 05
Impact

Pragmatic wins now, strategic findings in the roadmap.

The home-screen redesign and Levels of the System shipped to customers, surfacing the views people were already navigating to. The 84% ETL finding did its own work in the background — it reframed the next product cycle’s strategic question from “what dashboards do we add?” to “how do we bring the off-platform work back inside?” The cascading-data-issues diagnosis gave the team a shared vocabulary for a class of problems they hadn’t previously named, and it got scoped into the data-engineering roadmap.

One thing that doesn’t show up in a metric: the methodology transferred. The UI designer I worked with had a Contextual Design pipeline they could run on their own afterward. That was, in retrospect, the most durable thing the engagement produced.

A few things I took with me.

Couple every insight with a concept. Research that surfaces structural truths without proposed fixes asks an organization to absorb a lot at once; pairing each finding with a sketchy concept turns “the product needs to change” into “here’s what one version of changing looks like.” Run the methodology end-to-end on a single user before pitching it to the org — showing the chain from interview to wireframe in one go made Contextual Design legible for a team that hadn’t worked with a designer before. And: the numbers in the brief are hypotheses, not facts. “Extremely low volume” was an internal estimate. Field research turned it into 84%.

What I’d do differently.

I’d bring concept fragments into the insight presentations on purpose. Walking leadership through a strategic research finding without an accompanying design sketch leaves the “now what?” question entirely on their plate. Now I prototype the concept while I’m still in the field, even rough, so the insight conversation carries straight into a design conversation.

Pair every insight with a concept. Research that surfaces structural truths without proposed fixes asks an organization to absorb a lot at once. The bigger the truth, the bigger the absorption cost.

Offset · Numo · PNC · Service Blueprinting, Strategy

Finding innovation in SBA loans.

A bank brief that explicitly excluded efficiency plays. A full-process service blueprint of an SBA loan, from origination to secondary-market sale. The unattended market sat right in the middle.

Role
Lead designer · partnered with a business lead on financial analysis
Context
Numo, the FinTech incubator at PNC
Methods
Stakeholder interviews, end-to-end service blueprinting, build-vs-buy analysis, consumer product concepting
Outcome
Concept, mockups, and roadmap handed to PNC’s loan engineering org

The bank wanted innovation, not efficiency. I was sent to look at Small Business Administration loans with a brief that explicitly excluded operational improvements. I took a business lead with me for the financial analysis.

— 01
Problem

Find innovation, not efficiency.

Numo, the FinTech incubator at PNC, was scoping new revenue lines that could spin out as standalone products. The brief excluded operational improvements, explicitly. The team wasn’t looking for a way to make existing processes 10% better; we were looking for a market the bank wasn’t currently in.

That’s a hard brief. “Innovation” without a target is unfundable. The work was to find one. SBA-guaranteed lending is a multi-billion-dollar asset class — anywhere a bank touches that flow but doesn’t monetize it is a candidate.

— 02
Research

An end-to-end service blueprint, drawn from interviews.

Dozens of stakeholder interviews — origination, underwriting, servicing, treasury, capital markets. An end-to-end service blueprint with actors, actions, and the document artifacts that move between them. A quantitative spread/yield analysis on the SBA-guaranteed loan portions. And a competitive scan of the existing secondary-market participants, so the proposal could speak to who we’d be playing against.

For each step of the loan I drew the actors as circles and the actions as boxes, starting when an SMB went looking for a loan and continuing through origination, the bank’s data gathering, servicing, and the eventual sale of the guaranteed portion to a secondary market. The diagram below is the simplified version.

Key finding — the unattended middle.

By the end of mapping, I had a hunch. The middle of the process — the secondary market — was where the bank wasn’t playing. Pool Assemblers chopped loans into credit-rated tranches and sold them to institutional buyers on Bloomberg terminals. PNC had every other piece of the chain in-house. Just not that one.

A quick analysis of the money on the table sized the opportunity. The unattended middle was the brief’s answer.

— 03
Process

Concept, distribution, build-vs-buy, internal narrative.

Design decision — consumer marketplace, not institutional tranche.

The concept: build a digital marketplace for the guaranteed portions of SBA loans slated for the assemblers — but offer them to consumers as investment vehicles, instead of to institutional buyers as tranches. Government-backed. Better risk-to-yield than most consumer-available products. Marketplace economics.

Distribution leverage from existing PNC services.

PNC offered loan-management services to smaller banks and credit unions. The new platform could ride that distribution. Smaller banks could route loans to our marketplace instead of the assemblers; loans the assemblers rejected as too small to be profitable could still go to consumer buyers. The proposition extended PNC’s existing relationships rather than competing with them.

Internal narrative — the one-pager.

A one-pager went to internal stakeholders to communicate the value and explain how future iterations would feed back into the bank’s other operations. Internal narratives matter. The deck-as-pitch lesson from Pitch Fest applied here too.

Build versus buy.

Back-of-the-napkin: build was 12–18 months, $1–3M to MVP. We identified Stackfolio as a candidate acquisition: existing transaction volume, multi-loan-type support, an existing sales force, in-house subject matter experts. Estimated valuation 4–6× adjusted EBITDA, ~$2M enterprise value.

The case for buy was that it mitigated the build risks. The case for build was that PNC had the product capability, the build cost was trivial against long-term upside, and ownership of the entire chain was the goal. The bank chose to build.

— 04
Solution

A site architecture and pixel-perfect mockups, handed to engineering.

The deliverable was a site architecture and a complete set of pixel-perfect mockups. The work was handed to the loan team’s engineering org for build.

In the front end: a searchable, refinable loan list, sortable by Offset Score and the fields a retail investor needs to compare. A single-loan detail view with borrower context, the lender’s pedigree, the SBA guarantee percentage, key terms, and a clock for auction or silent-bid mechanics. The Offset Score itself was a composite credit-and-risk indicator made readable for non-institutional buyers. And both auction and silent-bid mechanics were baked into the same listing template, so the platform could carry both transaction styles without forking.

— 05
Impact

A new market, identified by service blueprint.

The unattended middle of the SBA loan flow was identified, sized, and pitched as a fundable new revenue line. The bank approved the build path. The loan team’s engineering org took ownership of the work. We handed off a complete site architecture and a pixel-perfect mockup set as the build foundation. The build-vs-buy analysis — including the Stackfolio acquisition assessment — gave leadership a documented basis for the decision they made.

A few things I took with me.

Service blueprints surface markets, not just inefficiencies. Drawing the existing chain end-to-end is a strategy tool when the brief is “find a place we’re not.” Distribution leverage is a design constraint — riding PNC’s existing loan-management relationships made the proposition cheaper to defend internally than a stand-alone bet would have been. And build-vs-buy is part of the design deliverable: a solid acquisition candidate (Stackfolio) made the build decision real, not lazy. Both options have to be sized for the choice to matter.

What I’d do differently.

I’d run the consumer-side concept past target retail investors before the build sizing. We sized the opportunity from PNC’s side; we didn’t test whether the buyers we expected to show up would actually find the Offset Score legible. A small consumer test would have either reinforced the case or surfaced UX risks the engineering team would have to absorb later.

The brief said “find innovation.” The process map said where it was already sitting, unbought. Most service blueprints surface efficiency opportunities. Sometimes they surface markets.

LikeNow · Consumer Marketplace, Field Research, Founding

Designing an app with friends.

A consumer marketplace for the multi-skill, social-media-fluent end of the on-demand economy. Shipped to web and to iOS beta. Built from interviews with the people the existing platforms can’t see.

Role
Co-founder · designer · led a team of 2 engineers and 1 designer
Collaborators
Joey Rahimi, Mike Darweesh, Megan Earfly
Stage
Shipped · web at likenow.org · iOS app in TestFlight beta
Themes
Platform design, labor economics, field research, the on-demand economy

LikeNow started in a single conversation. Joey had been thinking about how startup “dogfooding” culture had silently excluded lower-income citizens from the ideas the on-demand economy was actually generating. I was working on a buy-back service for used electronics, with a customer base in lower-income neighborhoods. After that conversation we joined forces, and expanded the concept from electronics to the full Craigslist economy.

— 01
Problem

An on-demand market that was structurally hostile to the demographic it claimed to serve.

The on-demand economy of the last decade promised flexible work to anyone with a phone. The reality was a class filter at the front — background checks, vehicle minimums, app-store account requirements — and a take rate at the back of 25–50% per transaction that dragged effective earnings below federal minimums for many workers. The people the platforms most loudly claimed to serve were the ones the platforms’ economics worked against.

Meanwhile, workers in the demographic we wanted to serve were running their own businesses on Instagram and getting filtered out of the platforms that would have officially “recognized” them. Customers who wanted to hire those workers were finding them on Instagram too. Not on the marketplaces. The demographic was real, the demand was real, and the existing platforms were systematically not for them. A market the bigger players didn’t see.

— 02
Research

Two field interviews reset what the platform was for.

A founder-led structural analysis of the on-demand economy first — class filters, take rates, who they exclude. Then field interviews with target workers in Pittsburgh, weeks apart. Then synthesis by pattern-matching across the interviews.

The structural critique came first. Class filters, take-rate as reverse minimum wage; we’ll come back to it. It told us why something like this should exist. The two field interviews told us what kind. Names changed.

Tasha. Dog walker. Thrift store. Paper-collage workshops. Instagram.

Tasha came in expecting us to ask about dog walking. That’s one of her jobs. We learned about the others fast. She manages a thrift store. She runs paper-collage workshops out of a borrowed studio. She has an Instagram following she’s spent years building, and every client across all three income streams arrives through that one channel.

She wasn’t waiting to be discovered. She was already running her own audience — fluent in the social-media world her customers were already living in.

We had come prepared to design for an underemployed person. We left knowing we were designing for a small business owner whose business happened to be three businesses, anchored by a single audience. The question wasn’t how does she get found. It was why does no platform represent her actual self.

Darrell. Minivan. Costco. Sprinter. $6,000 a month.

The first thing Darrell told us about was the minivan. The minivan was the start. The Sprinter came an hour into the conversation.

The product was bulk candy. He bought it at Costco and sold it in Wilkinsburg, mostly to neighborhood kids. He had a route. The route had margins. The most pressing thing on his mind was an app — specifically, an app that would let kids in the neighborhood see where his van was in real time, the way you check whether the ice-cream truck has turned the corner.

We came in expecting to hear about a side hustle. We heard about $6,000 a month and a customer service problem.

— What both interviews shared

Most of Darrell’s revenue, and a meaningful share of Tasha’s, never touched a card terminal. The industry datasets on the on-demand economy don’t see any of it. They see Uber. They see DoorDash. They don’t see Tasha. They don’t see Darrell. They don’t see the room they’re standing in.

— 03
Process

Two patterns, three design constraints.

The system context.

The standard on-demand model excludes lower-income workers in two ways at once. Background checks and vehicle minimums act as a class filter — driving for Uber requires a car worth more than some people’s annual salary. Even when those filters clear, platform take rates of 25–50% function as a reverse minimum wage, dragging effective earnings below federal minimums.

That argument is why a platform like this should exist. The interviews told us what it should be.

Pattern 01 — Multiplicity.

Tasha was the cleanest case. People in this market have skills and jobs in the plural. The current on-demand market forces a worker to be a single thing — a driver, a tasker, a courier — and workers respond rationally by installing four apps and switching between them through the day. None of those apps know each other. None of them know that the person driving right now is the same person who runs a thrift store and a workshop series.

The platform’s job is to elevate the worker’s range, not flatten it.

Pattern 02 — An invisible economy.

Darrell makes $6,000 a month. That money is real. It is not visible to industry analysts because it doesn’t transit a card processor. The local cash economy is alive — and any platform that can only see card-present transactions is seeing a fraction of the labor market it thinks it’s describing.

The point isn’t to bank everyone or to make the cash legible to the IRS. The point is to design for the people who already live in this economy, on their terms.

Three constraints follow.

  • Respect multiplicity. A profile is a small portfolio, not a single vertical. Workers add, remove, and reorder skills as their lives change.
  • Don’t extract platform-scale rents. Stay lean. Keep the team small. Take rates set the ceiling on every worker’s earnings; the take rate is a values statement.
  • Build for people who already have an audience. The supply side of this market is full of small operators who carry their own demand — people who get social media. The platform’s job is to amplify their reach, not gatekeep it. On the buy side, the same people are already finding the hip plumber on Instagram. The platform should make that pattern legible instead of fighting it.
— 04
Solution

A consumer marketplace, shipped to web and iOS beta.

The concept set covered the multi-skill worker profile, the request-and-acceptance flow, and a fee structure documented in plain language on the worker’s dashboard rather than buried in terms. The point of the screens was to make the values visible. A platform that hides its take rate is making one kind of design choice; a platform that shows it on every paystub is making another.

The worker profile is a small portfolio — delivery, hauling, painting, errand running, all on one page, ranked and verified. The search URLs are sentences the user might say out loud: find a masseuse, find a tow truck, find a painter. The same worker can appear under several. Workers can toggle availability between working, on vacation, and on a job without going through support. Buyers can see who is actually around right now on a real-time map. The take rate is a flat 8%, visible on every transaction; no surprise fees. And photos from past jobs sit next to the rating, so the rating is never the whole story.

What shipped.

We launched a marketing site at likenow.org and pushed an iOS app to beta on TestFlight. I led a small team — two engineers and one designer — through the build. The web side carried discovery; the iOS app focused on the worker side.

Beta means beta. The app sat in TestFlight, not the App Store. It was a working artifact — a thing we put in front of interviewees so they could tell us what we got wrong before we went public.

— 05
Impact

A working marketplace, in front of the people it was built for.

The marketing site went live at likenow.org with the search-by-need URL pattern in production. The iOS app shipped to TestFlight beta with the availability toggles, real-time map, and Instagram-fluent profile editor. I led a team of three — two engineers and one designer — through design and build. The 8% flat take rate ended up documented on every worker dashboard. The design constraint, visible in production. And workers like Tasha and Darrell got shown the beta as part of validating the multi-skill profile and the cash-friendly economics.

A few things I took with me.

A platform’s economics are a design choice. Acting like they aren’t is also a design choice — just not an honest one. The 8% take rate on every paystub was the most consequential design decision in the product. The supply side carries its own audience — designing for workers who already had Instagram followings reframed the marketplace as an amplifier rather than a gatekeeper. And field interviews trump structural arguments. The critique told us why; the interviews told us what. The product without the interviews would have been a less honest artifact.

What I’d do differently.

I’d ship the web search-by-need pattern earlier and use it as a discovery instrument before the iOS app entered TestFlight. The web side was the place workers could land their existing Instagram audiences without having to convince anyone to install a beta. We had the right idea on the web. We should have leaned on it harder before fronting the iOS investment.

A platform’s economics are a design choice. Acting like they aren’t is also a design choice — just not an honest one.
